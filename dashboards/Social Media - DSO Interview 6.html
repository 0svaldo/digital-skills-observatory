Opening new social media accounts


New social media are hardly being explored, but respondents have been asked this question for 4 consecutive interviews so a drop-off is to be expected. A small portion of the respondents (5%) now reported to have opened a new social media account since the last interview, (about a month ago). During the previous interview this was 10%, before that 13.5% and before that 18%.

There was a wide spread of types of social media accounts opened by the respondents (8 different types in total). Instagram tops the list this time, but only with a small margin and no trend or outlier is shown throughout the interviews.

Using social media


The respondents are active social media users but their reporting of their social media use has been slightly dropping with each interview. During the 6th interview 80% said they have used social media since the last interview, about a month ago. The previous interview this percentage was 88%, before that 91% and before that 92%.

The minor differences between male and female, and treatment and control respondents have persisted during the latest interview. Men report being active on social media in slightly greater proportion than women, as do treatment respondents compared to control respondents. The difference between treatment and control is more distinct (and has consistently been distinct throughout the interviews): 84% of the treatment group reported social media use in the past month, against 78% of the control group.

Facebook and messenger apps still demand the attention of the biggest portion of the respondents. Each of these social media were mentioned by 125 and 110 respondents respectively. Gmail, Instagram and Twitter followed, being mentioned by 66, 43 and 22 respondents respectively. Comparisons by gender or group assignment show no differences here.

A sizeable group of respondents (30 people in total) have also stopped using some social media accounts since the last interview. 

More women than men have stopped using some social media accounts (24% vs 14%) and this is in line with the results from the previous interviews. Slightly more respondents from the treatment group than from the control group have stopped using some social media (22% vs 16%).

The same group of social media accounts that featured in the answers to previous questions make up the list of accounts that some respondents stopped using. Facebook and Messenger apps are mentioned most often (16 and 14 times respectively).

Learning about Social Media

Insert paragraph here...

Respondents consistently have shown a declining desire to learn about new specific social media over the different interviews. Some 60 respondents (38%) now say they do have a specific social media they want to learn about. During the previous interview this was 41%, before that 46%, and before that 58%.

A comparison by gender shows men and women responded about the same to this question. A comparison by group assignment shows respondents from the treatment group responded positively to this question more often than those from the control group: 41% versus 35% replied yes.

Answers to the question if they had learned anything new from their recent use of social media were coded. This shows that the majority of respondents (93 people) said they did not learn anything new. The remaining 34 respondents' answers mostly involved learning something about (using) messenger apps, social media, video calls or email.

A comparison by group assignment shows a marked difference between control and treatment. A bigger portion of the control group indicated they had learned something new from their social media use than of the treatment group: 35% versus 25%. This difference between the 2 groups has been flip-flopping throughout the interviews and no clear trend or reliable difference between control and treatment can be seen overall.
