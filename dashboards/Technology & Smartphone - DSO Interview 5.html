Use of Technology


A considerable portion of the respondents (40% or 66 respondents) indicated their use of technology or smartphone had changed since the last interview, about a month ago.

Men said their use of technology changed in far bigger numbers than women: 42 male respondents (almost 47% of all men) said yes, against 24 female respondents (31% of all women)

Likewise, a comparison by group assignment shows a considerable difference between control and treatment. While 52% of the treatment group said their use of technology had changed, only 27% of the control group said the same.

The "change" in use of their smartphone predominantly meant more use of their smartphone. Fifty-nine respondents said they have been using their phone more, while 32 respondents said they have been using their phone less.

The men have been using more of their smartphone in larger numbers than the women: 37 male respondents, or 43% of all men, said they had made more use of their phone, while only 22 female respondents, 29% of all women, said the same. Meanwhile, the proportion of women that have been using their phone less is greater than that of the men: 17 women, 22% of all women, said they had used their smartphone less, against 15 men, or 17% of all men.

Issues with Technology


While there is quite a variety in the types of problems encountered by the respondents while using their smartphones, most of them relate to the performance of the phone itself (or the network it uses).

Battery problems, a slow or hanging phone, a broken phone screen or storage problems are mentioned often. Similarly, network problems are an important issue encountered by the respondents.

Another important issue mentioned was the cost of using the phone. Fourteen respondents said their smartphone was expensive to use (a number that is relatively low, making up less than 10% of all respondents). 

Only a handful respondents mentioned problems they were having that related to their use of and familiarity with their smartphone. Issues with downloading videos, making screen shots or using email are some examples.

While there are some differences between the male and female respondents in the problems that were expressed, all of the recurring issues are mentioned by men and women alike and the same issues feature in both the men's and women's top 3: Hanging phones, network problems and the cost of using.

The biggest difference between the male and female respondents can actually be seen in the proportion of respondents stating they had encountered no issues at all. While 37 men (39%) answered "Nothing", 43 women (52%) did the same.

Helping others with Technology


Three quarter of all respondents have helped others use their phone, the internet or technology in general. This is considerably more than during the previous interviews, when half of all respondents said they had helped others.

Men far more often help others than women do: 81% of all men versus 68% of all women. A similar difference also exists between the control and treatment groups: 81% of the treatment helped others, versus 69% of the control group. These differences between the genders and the groups are in line with last the results from the last interview.

Friends or family are the most common beneficiaries of this help.
